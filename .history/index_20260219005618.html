Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Dataset

This dataset contains statistics describing corporate ownership rates and owner-occupancy rates for residential properties across Boston neighborhoods from 2004 to 2024. The data was derived from property assessment records provided by the City of Boston. The dataset contains both temporal and spatial components. The temporal dimension is represented by yearly observations spanning twenty years, while the spatial dimension is represented by individual Boston neighborhoods. For each neighborhood and year, the dataset records the proportion of housing units that are owner-occupied (own_occ_rate) and the proportion that are corporately owned (corp_own_rate). Additional census-based demographic attributes such as population of different races and neigh are also included. This dataset is relevant to our questions surrounding housing affordability because corporate ownership of residential properties has been linked to housing speculation, rising housing prices, and decreased housing stability for residents.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How have corporate ownership rates changed over time?

    The motivation behind this question is to gain a general understanding of when corporate ownership began to emerge as a significant issue and how rapidly it has grown. This helps establish the broader context of our investigation. Additionally, if we observe a clear increase in corporate ownership over time, it would suggest that housing is increasingly being treated as a financial asset by corporations, reinforcing the idea that real estate is being actively exploited for investment purposes.

  2. What is the relationship between corporate ownership and owner occupancy rates?

    The readings suggest that increasing corporate ownership may contribute to displacement by making housing less affordable for individual residents. As housing prices increase, fewer residents may be able to afford homeownership, leading to lower owner occupancy rates. Therefore, we want to investigate whether neighborhoods with higher corporate ownership tend to have lower owner occupancy rates.

  3. Which neighborhoods have experienced the greatest increases in corporate ownership?

    This question helps us identify whether corporate ownership is concentrated in specific neighborhoods or evenly distributed across the city. By examining where the largest increases have occurred, we can better understand which communities may be most affected by corporate investment and potentially more vulnerable to housing affordability challenges.

Discoveries & Insights

The dataset draws on authoritative sources (City of Boston property assessments and, in the Census file, block-group Census data) and is structured consistently over 2004–2024 with clear neighborhood-year units and decimal rates. Data quality is limited in several ways. The metadata for the Census and corporate ownership file states that corporate ownership is identified by keyphrase matching on owner names (e.g., LLC, LLP, Corp, Realty, Trust, Bank), which is incomplete and can misclassify both corporate and non-corporate owners. It also explains that owner-occupancy and corporate-ownership rates exclude blank or missing values from their denominators, so the rates are conditional on non-missing flags and may not represent all properties in a neighborhood. In the Census and corporate ownership file, the columns after own_occ_rate and corp_own_rate (Census variables such as total units, vacancy, and demographics) are constant across years within each neighborhood, likely because this information was captured in a single census year and then repeated. To account for this limitation, we do not use these Census variables to compare change over time; we use them only for comparisons across districts (neighborhoods) within a given snapshot.

Summary

In this exploratory data analysis, we examined how corporate ownership of residential housing has evolved across Boston neighborhoods from 2004 to 2024 and its relationship to owner occupancy and neighborhood characteristics. We first assessed data quality and found that the dataset provides consistent temporal and spatial coverage, though corporate ownership classification may contain some misclassification due to keyword-based identification. Our analysis shows that corporate ownership increased substantially in every Boston neighborhood over the period, while owner occupancy declined. This inverse relationship suggests a shift in housing from owner-occupied units toward corporate and investment ownership. We also found that corporate ownership growth came partly at the expense of individual landlord ownership, indicating broader restructuring of housing ownership patterns. Spatial analysis revealed that corporate ownership is highest in neighborhoods such as South Boston Waterfront, Fenway, and Downtown, while the largest percentage increases occurred in previously lower-corporate-ownership areas such as West Roxbury and East Boston. Finally, corporate ownership is negatively associated with neighborhood size and positively associated with vacancy rates, and it is more prevalent in neighborhoods with higher White and Asian populations compared to neighborhoods with higher Black and Latino populations. Overall, these findings suggest that corporate ownership has become an increasingly dominant feature of Boston’s housing market and may contribute to changes in housing stability, affordability, and neighborhood inequality.
